What is this?

Pikkukoski is a popular river beach on the Vantaanjoki, in the Oulunkylä area of Helsinki. Like all official Finnish bathing spots, it’s tested a handful of times each summer for two types of bacteria that indicate faecal contamination — sewage or animal waste getting into the water:

  • Intestinal enterococci (“Entero”)
  • E. coli

Both are measured in CFU per 100 mL (colony-forming units — roughly, “how many live bacteria would grow from a 100 mL water sample”). Under the EU Bathing Water Directive and Finnish guidelines for this type of inland water, a sample is considered fine to swim in below 400 CFU/100 mL for Enterococci and 500 CFU/100 mL for E. coli — that’s what “the limit” and the traffic-light colours on this dashboard refer to. Above it, the water is classed as poorer quality and swimming is more likely to make you sick (usually a stomach bug, not anything dangerous, but unpleasant).

The problem this dashboard tries to help with: the city only samples Pikkukoski every couple of weeks, so on any given day you’re usually looking at two-week-old lab results. Bacteria counts, though, can swing a lot faster than that — heavy rain washes contamination into the river (via runoff and overloaded sewers) and levels can spike within a day or two, then fade as the river flushes it out.

What the model actually does: it’s a statistical model trained on past lab samples together with each day’s rainfall and river flow, so it can “nowcast” — estimate today’s likely bacteria level — for every day in between real lab samples, using whatever rain/flow data is available right now. It is not a lab measurement; it’s an educated, uncertain guess.

How to read the “P(≤ limit)” percentages: these say how confident the model is that a real sample taken today would come back under the safety limit. 90% means “if we actually tested today, we’d expect it to pass about 9 times out of 10 based on recent rain and river conditions” — not “there’s a 90% chance the water is clean” in some absolute sense, and not a substitute for an actual test. Lower percentages mean more uncertainty or more risk, not a certainty of failure.

Bottom line: treat this as a rough, automated heads-up between official samples — useful for noticing “it rained hard yesterday, maybe be cautious” — not as an authority on whether the water is currently safe. For that, check the City of Helsinki’s own bathing water pages.

What the model predicts

Bacteria levels (Enterococci and E. coli, in CFU/100 mL) at Pikkukoski beach are nowcast — estimated for today and recent days from rain and river-flow data, ahead of the next lab sample — using a separate Bayesian regression for each bacteria type, fit with brms/Stan on June–August measurements only (the swimming season; the beach isn’t monitored the rest of the year):

log(bacteria) ~ rain_decay + log_flow + s(day_of_year, k = 6) + (1 | year) Predictors
  • rain_decay — trailing rainfall, exponentially weighted (3-day half-life), capturing short-lived combined-sewer-overflow pulses after rain.
  • log_flow — same-day Vantaanjoki river flow at Oulunkylä, capturing watershed-wide wetness and dilution.
  • s(day_of_year) — a smooth within-season trend (e.g. warmer water → more growth).
  • (1 | year) — a per-year random intercept for variation not explained by flow/rain.
Why Student-t, not Normal

Bacteria counts occasionally spike far above what rain/flow alone predicts (e.g. one-off contamination events, or censored “>800”/“>2400” lab readings). A Student-t likelihood has heavier tails than a Normal, so a handful of extreme readings pull the fitted trend line much less than they would under ordinary least-squares — the model stays representative of typical conditions instead of being dragged toward rare outliers.

Fitting and uncertainty

Each model is fit with 4 MCMC chains via Stan’s No-U-Turn sampler. Weakly informative priors (Normal/Student-t, centred at 0) regularise the coefficients given the fairly small number of summer samples per year. After fitting, the pipeline checks for divergent transitions — a warning sign that the sampler couldn’t reliably explore the posterior — and automatically refits with a smaller step size (higher adapt_delta) if any are found, up to 5 attempts, so the coefficients and intervals below can be trusted. The shaded bands on the Overview charts and the 90% CI columns below are 90% posterior credible intervals, not simple standard errors; the “P(≤ limit)” value boxes are the posterior predictive probability that a fresh sample today would fall under the regulatory limit.

Data sources

This dashboard combines three independently published datasets:

  • Rainfall — Finnish Meteorological Institute (FMI) open data, daily precipitation from the Helsinki Kumpula observation station (FMISID 101004), via the FMI WFS API.
  • River flow — SYKE / Finnish Environment Institute Hydrologiarajapinta, daily discharge from the Vantaanjoki river gauge at Oulunkylä (station ID 1097), via the Hydrologiarajapinta OData API.
  • Bacteria measurements — City of Helsinki bathing water quality reports (“Uimavedenlaatu”), lab samples for Pikkukoski beach on the Vantaanjoki river, published as an annual PDF report by the city’s environmental services.

Rain, river-flow, and bacteria data all update automatically and daily. New bacteria readings are applied automatically too, guarded by layout sanity checks (the report’s layout is not fully consistent year to year) — see R/00_fetch_data.R for details.
